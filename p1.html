Hokie Accomodation Portal System

Human Computer Interaction Project

Project

Hokie Accommodation Portal System (HAPS) is a web application that is intended to replace the current process of arranging alternate exam times for students with learning disabilities at Virginia Tech.   Demo

Context

The Services for Students with Disabilities (SSD) office serves more than two thousand students in Virginia Tech, providing various accommodations and services for students with learning disabilities. One accommodation that we identified to be commonly used are timed test extensions. The current process of arranging alternate exam times requires multi-communication between students, teachers, and the SSD office. This communication is usually done through emails or in person. From our interviews and research we found that this process lacks structure and often leads to points of confusion and frustration. Therfore the purpose of HAPS is to streamline the current process of arranging alternate exam times with the SSD office into an online scheduling system.The overall goal was to create a simple and direct communication line between all parties involved in arranging alternate exam times at Virginia Tech.

Design Process

Analyze   Design   Prototype   Evaluate

We followed the user-centered design process to determine how students with disabilities may want to improve their experience arranging alternate exam times based on observations and interviews.

Brainstorming: Letting user research be our guide, we extracted and grouped interviews and observations into an Affinity Diagram. We then analyzed our findings and identified interaction design requirements.

Interaction Design Requirements
Check-in: Students shall have the capability to check-in their arrival to take exams.
Schedule: Students should be able to post their schedules in this application so that teachers can easily find timings that best coordinate with their schedules and/or the SSD office schedule.
DM Inbox: Students, Teachers, SSD office workers shall have the ability to directly communicate with each other one on one or three-way.
Alternate Room Avaliability: SSD office workers shall have the capability to to list alternate testing room areas on the application.
User Profile: Students shall be able to create a profile that will list their special needs and be able to upload their accommodation approval papers.

Storboarding: Using all of the fleshed out ideas from the ideation and creation process, critique phase and mental models, we developed storyboards to showcase our application design and system.

Wireframing: We developed our wireframes in tandem with our storyboards to ensure continuity and improvement of user experience. We went through several iterations between the wireframes and storyboards until we achieved a model that we believed users would easily be able to use.

Prototyping: We began our prototype phase by developing key questions that we had based on the previous two phases and the purpose of the application. The questions outlined what we wanted our prototype to accomplish, including: achieving the intended purpose, appealing to both of our user bases, and being easy to use. In the end, we finalized four key questions:

  1. Does our system do a good job of giving fair capabilities for all intended users?
  2. Does our application’s target users have the skill set to navigate the application?
  3. Does our application do justice in streamlining the alternative testing process that is currently in place?
  4. Is our application’s creation of visual representations of communication appealing?
Based on our key questions, we chose to use a T type, click-through, medium-high fidelity prototype. This is intended to give a large, shallow look of all the features (Calendar, Direct Messenger, User Profile, and Student Listings), but in particular, it would dive in deep to showcase our main feature: exam scheduler.

Evaluation Plan: For our UX Evaluation process, we determined that a mixture of the cognitive walkthrough approach and heuristic evaluation would be best for evaluating our system. Throughout the evaluation, we also used the think aloud protocol, asking the users to share their thought process while completing the task. Our evaluation consisted of two members of our team conducting the evaluation with two expert participants. The procedure was conducted in two phases to evaluate our application’s student and faculty formats. The first phase was the evaluation of our student view of HAPS. The second phase was the evaluation of our professor view of HAPS. In both phases, we followed relatively the same process. We began by giving our experts a sheet with tasks to complete using our system, such as “book this appointment” or “check this accommodation”. At the same time, we gave our experts a copy of Nielson’s Heuristics, and a heuristic violation form to fill out if any violations were discovered through the process of completing the aforementioned tasks.

Findings: Our evaluation went smoothly with only a few small issues with our prototype that were revealed through the evaluation. The most common violation that was reported was “User control and freedom”, often in reference to small visual mistakes in our system that need to be corrected to ensure users can operate the system with ease. One violation we took special notice of was a Consistency and Standards violation which stated, “In user profile, accommodation history is not indicated as private, while the personal forms is.” This was crucial to fix as user privacy is key in a confidential system such as HAPS. Some positive feedback was given in response to questions regarding the visual appeal and intuitiveness of the prototype design.
